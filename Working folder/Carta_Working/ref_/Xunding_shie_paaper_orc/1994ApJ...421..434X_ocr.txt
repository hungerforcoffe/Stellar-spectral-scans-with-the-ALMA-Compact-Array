1. INTRODUCTION

Knowledge of the physical conditions of the interstellar gas
in galaxies is fundamental to understanding the processes of
stellar and galactic evolution since these conditions determine
the nature of star formation. Due to the difficulties of observing
molecular hydrogen emission on the ground, the physical conditions of the molecular gas are mainly inferred from observations of trace molecules such as CO and CS. In view of the fact
that rotational lines of different molecules usually have either
different excitation requirements or opacities, the physical conditions ascertained by a multiline study are needed to provide
a more complete view of the interstellar medium. In this paper
we present observations of the nuclear regions of the galaxies
NGC 2146 and IC 342 in 12C0 and `3C0 J = 2-1 and 1-0, as
well as CS J = 2-1 lines, using the FCRAO 14 m telescope. We
show here the first `2C0 and 13C0 J = 2-1 spectra in the
center and disk of NGC 2146, and report the first detection of
CS J = 2-1 emission from NGC 2146.
NGC 2146 is a peculiar Sab spiral galaxy. Its complex
optical morphology (Pease 1920; de Vaucouleurs 1950; Bevenuti, Capaccioli, & D'Odorico 1975; Young et al. 1988a;
Young, Kleinmann, & Allen 1988b) and strong nuclear radio
continuum and CO emission (Condon et al. 1982; Young et al.
1988a; Solomon & Sage 1988) suggest that it is experiencing a
phase of violent star-forming activity. IC 342 is a nearby luminous Scd spiral galaxy. Strong CO, infrared, and radio continuum emission from the nuclear region of IC 342 (e.g., Morris &
Lo 1978; Becklin et al. 1980; Knapp et al. 1980; Rickard &
Palmer 1981 ; Young & Scoville 1982; Turner & Ho 1983;
Hayashi et al. 1986; Eckart et al. 1990; Sage & Solomon 1991)
indicate enhanced star-forming activity, while interferometric
`2C0 f = 1-0 observations disclose bar or armlike structures
